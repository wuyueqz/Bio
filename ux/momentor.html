MoMentor

A mobile solution to improve MIT students' mentorship experience in career consultation and development

Problem

Getting practical career advice from online mentors is a crucial experience for university students. However, MIT's existing product only provides a web-based application for the mentorship with a limited matching matrix. The Career Advising and Professional Development Office (CAPD) wanted to propose a mobile application that could compensate the existing mentorship product, and improve the mentoring experience.

Get a sense of the existing problems of mentorship platform through the preliminary survey

According to a preliminary study about the Alumni Hub, I synthesized the responses from 60 students and found that the existing product can't 1) match students with the best mentors; 2) ensure the in-person meeting; 3) guarantee in-time reply. Therefore, I explored mobile alternatives to provide a better experience for MIT students' career consultancy with mentors.

The service that MIT's existing product Alumni Advisor's Hub fails to provide

Solution

A mobile application MoMentor was prototyped and tested with target students and presented to the career advising office to inform the future design and improvement of the school's mentorship platform.

A quick overview of designed key screens (matching, distance filtering, and reminder sending) of MoMentor

Process

When it comes to designing, I followed a five-step design process from User Research, Synthesize, Design, Prototype, to Evaluate. The preliminary product study helps to identify the user research's direction. Each step takes valuable advice and feedback from MIT CAPD's officer.

Research

I began by designing detailed user research, to clearly understand the problems with the mentorship product. I used an online questionnaire to gather information. The questionnaire is designed in a binary structure for both mentors and mentees. I also researched potential solutions, e.g. the most comfortable time to send a reply reminder.

Survey

Distributed online to the MIT community with 70 responses

Interview

Conducted experience interview with 5 students

Competitive Analysis

Looked at the existing web app and other mobile apps

Survey

The survey, which can be accessed here, was distributed through undergrad dorm, graduate school department, career development mailing list, alumni network, and social media. It asked questions regarding mentor and mentee's career background, consultancy preference/behavior, past mentorship experience, and asked the potential solutions in advance.

The design of the survey

From their answers about the negative mentorship experience, we understood that people were primarily concerned about: 1) the matching accuracy; 2) the in-person interaction; 3) the response efficiency. These briefly align with my preliminary research. Some key quotes include:

“I couldn't change my advisor recommended by the system-it had limited filters.”

“The system assigned me a mentor in Seattle, which made it impossible for us to meet in person.”

“The mentors did not always get back to me in time. I guess mostly they just forgot.”

Interview

Parallel to the survey, I conducted 5 rounds of the in-person interview with students from different backgrounds and career mentorship needs, and 5 rounds of the in-person interview with mentors from different industrial sectors to help get their perspective on how the mobile product might solve for those concerns.

Interview questions focus on the more descriptive experience

Instead of asking the quantitive data, I invited the users to share the best and worst mentorship experience and tried to figure out the reason behind it. At the end of the interview, I collected the concerns they had expressed and created the persona for everyone for further grouping and analysis.

Collect the information and created the persona for interviewees

Competitive Analysis

I also a conducted holistic analysis of the existing web application and other mobile mentorship application. Understanding the pros and cons of the web application contributed to guiding the mobile design further.

Case study of the Alumni Hub

SWOT analysis of the Alumni Hub

Among the product studied, 1) to improve matching: the Everwise and River provide a quick onboard process and can adjust the matching algorithm according to career goal, while the MentorCliQ can also show the matching degree; 2) to ensure the time commitment: MentorCliQ can rank the mentors by their time commitment, while Chronus is able to track each mentors' time in program; 3) to encourage in-person meeting, Chronus allows users to schedule in-person appointment. I will incorporate and test some practical and successful ideas into the app design.

Using the matching/time/in-person matrix to evaluate the existing products

Synthesize

Once I gathered all the results, analyzed the data and generated insights from the holistic research, which can be accessed here, I mapped them in order to validate the preliminary hypothesis about the three key objectives, brainstormed and co-designed with the career advisor from MIT regarding the potential solutions that could be used to guide design. In the end, we had three main methods corresponding to our three objectives:

Matching Accuracy

Quick Wizard before matching process according to survey

In-person Interaction

Add distance matrix to provide an alternative for offline meet-up

Time Commitment

Send reminders to mentors in appropriate time

Matching Accuracy

On the one hand, I aggregated the most concerned features that students care about defining a mentor, including the career goal, industrial sector, mentorship identity preference, target service, format preference. These features will lead to the future design of matching wizard.

On the other hand, 4 out of 5 students mentioned sometimes they might only have quick questions. In this way, posting a question on a public channel can raise the success of finding a matched mentor/answer. Therefore, providing a quick question-based mentor matching alternative is also a practical solution.

Brainstorming with stakeholders from CAPD office to identify the key matching features

A look at aggregating the options of the features for the wizard

In-person Interaction

Using a distance-based matrix is a practical way of ensuring the offline meeting in many matching applications. Showing the distance matrix can help the mentees find mentors in the same city. After discussing with the stakeholders, we decided to include a background check for the verified users (i.e. link the student's account to school's system) to solve the security problem regarding showing the geolocation of the mentors.

Providing the option of in-person coffee chat during the on-boarding matching process also increase the propensity of the off-line meeting between mentors and mentees.

Time Commitment

Sending a customized reminder to mentees who forget to reply in time is a practical way in many social applications. During the interview with mentors, all the mentors are acceptable or even welcome the reminders. The most acceptable time for the mentors to receive another reminder is around 1 week, which needs to be tested further.

Ranking mentors' time invested is another potential solution. However, during the conversation with CAPD and interviewees, I realized that it might cause some emotional side-effect, due to that most of the mentors, as alumni, were volunteered to offer help. Using the time to quantify their contribution would highly likely hurt them.

The following was the interim result of the low-fidelity screens and wireframe after a couple of iterations, which corresponds to my sketch.

The low-fidelity screens/wireframe design

Let's see how this structure and design helps to streamline the mentoring experience. To achieve the quick and customized matching, the wizard helps to identify mentee's mentor preference during onboarding. I applied the features for filters synthesized before to facilitate this experience. Mentee's are also allowed to go back or skip questions, or skip the wizard at the very beginning - however, there is an inline card that repeats the value of the wizard, recommending mentee to finish the questionnaire.

The onboarding process setting up the matching matrix

After loading the matched mentor list, the mentee can explore based on location and match degree. It is also able to change the preference by changing the Likert chart.

After selecting a mentor, the mentee is able to book a conversation/submit resume/send quick questions - these functions are also identified by previous user research. The conversation option allows three potential time, with options for in-person meeting, which helps achieve offline interaction. Mentors are allowed to recommend other mentors for mentee during chatting.

The customized matching, quick inquiry, and recommendation

I opted for bottom navigation with 3 views: Dashboard where you can find coming conversations and saved mentors/system recommended mentors; Online videos for career development; Public questions where you can post public questions and allow all mentors to answer. I won’t go into detail for the tutorials and public questions since they work much the same as in other apps.

Besides, one thing I find important is that when posted a public question, system can send notifications to potential mentors who can answer the question without taking so much time.

3 views: dashboard, webinar, and forum

To help ensure the time commitment, I also introduced the reminder mechanism in the setting. Once a request/question has not been replied/answered after 1 week, the duration of which is identified by user research, the system will send a reminder notification for mentors. This message can also be customized.

Evaluate

User Test

Once I had the prototype, it was time to see how they performed with people. I rounded up willing participants to go through the user testing and get their impressions on this mentorship application demo. The feedback would provide guideline for further development.

Participants

Cover 9 students, both undergrad/grad students with career advising needs

Think—aloud protocols

Document the user's impression in every step

Metrics Measured

Effectiveness, Efficiency, Satisfaction

All 9 participants finished the test objectives. However, during the test, I observed several problems for potential improvement. For example, 8 out of 9 participants mentioned that the Likert chart above the mentor list for filtering visualization and editing was confusing. One participant with technical background also mentioned that it could be technically difficult for implementing this visual filter.

Testing out the prototype

After the test, I had all the participants fill out a questionnaire containing some short answer questions and a prompt to rate the objectives on a Likert scale for three metrics:

  • Effectiveness (Could you finish the task successfully?)
  • Efficiency (How long do you spend finding the solution?)
  • Satisfaction (How much did you want to use it?)

Results

To analyze the data, I compiled the Likert scores and compared medians across a wide margin of metrics. The graphs below are an average across all metrics of each objective with matching, scheduling, and reminding.

Results for the Likert chart

From these charts, it is easy to see that the users had high performance in effectiveness and efficiency, and expressed the willingness of using this experience.

We do notice that there was a slight drop in effectiveness about off-line meeting appointment. Digging into the reason, one user mentioned that it was because that the distance matrix is shown in the list instead of during the wizard. However, most of the users did notice this function and regarded it as a practical way to find mentors around them.

One other important result was that the matching efficiency was lower compared with the other sectors. 3 user mentioned that it was because:

“I think to adjust the filter on the circle with limited space on the screen is not so convenient and will cause inaccuracy.”

During the test, some users also mentioned that they were concerned and cautious about sending a reminder to strangers especially the mentors who are volunteering offering help. This was why they might not choose to use the reminder function. This explains the drop of satisfaction in reminding session.

Refinements and Recommendations

From my observations, testing, and participant feedback, I then refined the design of the filtering to a more user-friendly way. I allow the users to go back and adjust the filter further instead of asking them to play with the Likert chart.

The improvements of the filtering process

Recall our intention of helping more students get appropriate career advice from mentors. I finally evaluated whether I achieved the 3 goals I set at the very beginning. By deploying different strategies below, I was able to achieve my goals.

Evaluate according to previous objectives

I presented the design proposal with the MIT CAPD. During the conversation regarding the further improvement with them, I eventually identified other design and non-design opportunities for system's long-term implementation and maintenance by this user journey mapping.

Future road map of the application

Lessons Learned

From this project, I learned a lot about the whole process of experience design. I scoped the project, consulted with the CAPD, and eventually presented the design proposal to the administrators with practical feedback and support for further development.

For a bi-party application, it is always very hard to start designing. In this way, taking one perspective (in this case as a mentee) helps to simplify the complicated problem, streamline the process, figure out the interaction, and scale up the product.
