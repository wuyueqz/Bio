Shinshilla

Empower the traditional real estate developer with digital community collaboration and informed urban planning platform in Ukraine

Problem

Corporations and public entities in Ukraine desire an easy and democratic way to gather community input, and Ukrainian citizens desire a platform for their feedback to be incorporated into the urban planning process rapidly unfolding in their communities. Developers and urban planners need access to data to inform their decisions.

What is the challenge that the project is faced with?

Solution

Shinshilla provides a united platform for different stakeholders in Ukraine to access existing urban planning data and information, exchange diverse crowdsourcing data, generate and collect new user data to measure how people use public and private spaces in their city, therefore inform urban planning, design, and community development strategies.

A quick overview of designed key screens of Shinshilla showing how to submit a project

Why Ukraine

Anastasiya Ponomaryova is a Ukrainian urbanist from a Kyiv-based non-profit urban design agency Urban Curator. During summer 2018, she organized an independent study of Ukraine postindustrial district redevelopment collaborating Ukrainian developer SAGA.

Both of our team members were interested in diagnosing the existing urban design problem and in researching/testing a participatory design methodology in this post-soviet country. After visiting our site Podil (a district more than 13 mi²), Kyiv on August 2018, we came back to MIT continuing the analysis of the original site observation and investigation (including building quality study, urban collective memory study) and proposed the idea of developing the urban design data collecting platform Shinshilla. Meanwhile, with the help of Urban Curator, we continued organizing surveys/interviews/user test in order to help us identify the real need for such a platform.

The timeline of the project

Process

The process of this project was divided into five steps, namely the qualitative research, the quantitative research, the co-design, the prototyping and the presenting. At the end of the project, the team of two delivered a conceptual data collection platform to the stakeholder, organized a participatory design exhibition with the local community, and presented the product design proposal during IDEAS social innovation challenge.

Qualitative Research

Observation

Ukraine is a new democracy and the landscape is rapidly changing with large investments in capital assets and a growing presence as a regional hub for tech and innovation. The Ukrainian government, private sector players and citizens are working together to strengthen their democracy and decide the future for their planning and community development initiatives.

The site visit of a real estate development project in Kyiv

In this time of exciting innovation, stakeholders expressed that there is a lack of reliable data available to government civic partners on the ground. Different stakeholders reported that they experience a lack of trust and collaboration in the public and private sector in order to share analytical insights and data sets that would inform their decision-making and ultimately ensure the design of a human-oriented built environment. Ukrainian citizens report that public spaces of historic and contemporary importance are being repurposed or disassembled without community input. It is projected that in the next ten years there will be massive investments in infrastructure, housing and public utilities in the region that will transform the urban landscape.

In order to understand what the citizens and the developers really need, I first conducted a series of qualitative research during on-site visit beyond observation.

Literature Review

I first looked into the literature review including academic papers to understand the historical factors that lead to this situation. I also looked into the journals and other informal publications to diagnose the problem. Additionally, I looked into the online dataset and understood that the long anarchic state leads to the trustlessness between the developers and the citizens.

The literature review sectors

Interview

Parallel to the literature review, I also conducted an interview with more than 10 local residents in order to understand their needs. Instead of requesting the quant data, I invited the residents to share their past experience and concerns during the urban planning process. At the end of the interview, I collected the information and included some key quotes:

“Our spontaneous creative activities are often neglected during the urban development process.”

“Lots of informal places are pulling down, which used to be the places for our daily gathering.”

“I feel that our precious informal memories are not fully respected and appreciated during the existing urban planning process.”

Competitive Analysis

Our preliminary research shows that our peers are doing what they can to gather/scrape data from social media channels and create community profiles for marketing purposes (e.g. Twitter):

"However, all of these methods are not transparent, or are done without citizen consent."

The competitive analysis of other existing products

In this way, we understood the necessity of proposing the data collection techniques/products/tools that are transparent and can deliver data in a way that will be used for urban planning initiatives by both private and public sector players.

Further, this preliminary research shows that if successful, our social impact could later expand to serve other countries in the region.

Social Media Study

After understanding the existing problem through observation and interview, as well as the limitation of existing product through comparative study, I want to understand who are the actual users of the space that SAGA Development is going to create for. Due to the absence of government agencies, Ukraine is lack of actual data, which can tell the country’s socio-demographic characteristics. The need of the data allows the study to seek an alternative for this data gap.

The data pipeline of online social media study

In this way, the information decoded from different informal social media platforms can tell how people actually use their urban space (user, location, time, duration, activity type, frequency, etc.), which provide a lens to mine the value (either to preserve the postindustrial heritage as social activity containers, or to reprogram it according to user’s actual need) of the Podil post-industrial area.

Therefore, I first created a visualization to summarize Podil people’s collective profile from Facebook (this process got user consent), to map people’s activity with the geolocations, to find the places in Podil that have the greatest potential to create social venue.

Co-design

Workshop

Taking the insights of the previous social media study, we organized two rounds of co-design exercise with different stakeholders including real estate developers and other local designers. We were targeting on figuring out:

  • What the traditional real estate development process is in Ukraine?
  • What SAGA need during the urban planning process?
  • What process can be streamlined and more transparent?
  • What data can be accessible to the general public?

During the interview, the co-design from SAGA is introducing the urban planning process in Ukraine for the audience

Among the mapping of the whole process, we realized that our main opportunity lies in the planning and design stage. During this stage, the developer would like to collect more data about the citizen’s urban living behavior and design preference. The information on new project release, design decision making, and construction bidding can be more transparent, i.e. the data can be accessible to the public.

Present

Showcase

Once we had the prototype, I participated in the annual IDEAS conference for social innovation. We showcased the prototype and interacted with the audience. The idea of Shinshilla was highly welcomed, and people also mentioned that in many U.S. cities, there's also a solid need for a participatory design tool for community engagement. Some quotes of the visitors as the following:

“It is an awesome idea and I really like this product! We should use it when they are trying to pull down that old bridge (the Longfellow Bridge)!”

I was presenting the demo of Shinshilla at the IDEAS conference for the public

Exhibition

After the showcase and test with the users, I also helped the Urban Curators to prepare the CAN Festival in Kyiv. I was involved in designing a digital and physical map installation to collect the visitors' sentimental opinion towards the places in Kyiv. Interface

During the exhibition, I also designed a Processing-based interface for the visitors to enter their opinions towards specific places in Kyiv. The project Map Me Happy helps to collect qualitative data. The color corresponds to the grading of the three-dimensional matrix - live, work, and play. I aimed to provide a playful experience for visitors to exchange ideas through this media. A design agency can also export dataset and perform further analysis.

Lessons Learned

Reflecting the nine-month design research regarding Podil’s future urban development, it is a unique learning experience-I identified the problem by the careful on-site observation/investigation and holistic user research and proposed a data-driven diagnose and human-centered design. Since the project is for the public, I also made all the steps and resources public and transparent.

The summary of my code and data

During the several rounds of the pitch, I also realized that the problem that I found can also be applied to other places. In this way, to make the product scalable is very important. In a further development, we can make the product as different modules, with the flexible ones responsive to specific conditions. We are currently raising funding from SAGA development and would like to see this project realized in the future.
