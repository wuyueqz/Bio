Colette

A product for Diabetes Type I patients making the right decisions on food shopping

Problem

Colette is a doctoral student at Tufts University. She has diabetes mellitus type I disease, which means she has trouble with digesting/absorbing glucose, thus can only eat a certain kind of food that has a low glycemic index (GI). When shopping, she has difficulty finding products with a minimal glycemic index.

The problem that Colette is faced with

To be specific, neither can she look up the GI number related to the product instantly, nor can she get a general idea of the average number of the GI number in her cart. In this way, she reached a group of MIT students to design a mobile application that can help the patients like her to make purchase decisions about the right products.

What Colette is thinking when shopping?

“I have had type 1 diabetes since I was 1.5 years old. I was diagnosed with ADHD in college. A low GI diet has been helpful to me in handling both these conditions.

I would like an app that can register and carb count the products to rank what is available in each store per product category.”

- Colette

Solution

After a month's diagnose and design, the group delivered a demo of the mobile application for Colette (we also named the application after our user's name), which was able to:

  • Monitor her daily glucose absorption;
  • Know the glucose index with different foods;
  • Calculate the average GI in the cart;
  • Help Colette to share GI knowledge with other people

Design of the key screens (dashboard, dictionary, calculator, forum)

Process

When it comes to the design process, there are two stages of development.

During the first stage, we were teaming up with Colette during a design hackathon, in which we did a detailed user study, synthesize the user need, and co-designed with her. We also sketched the wireframe and completed a high-fidelity design of the product's key screens at this stage. We did a presentation at the end out the Hackathon, with Colette showcasing the application and get feedback from different stakeholders.

Secondly, we tested the original prototype with multiple users and evaluated the feedback. We revised design regarding both the experience and visual components. Colette also took the actions in terms of raising funding for further implementing it.

Research

Designing for a medical product is very complicated and should be cautious. Since diabetes mellitus type I is a new topic to me, I follow a four-step research process in order to clarify our problem, summarize our scope and set design objectives.

Literature Review

Conducted literature review regarding the problem

Interview

Conducted detailed interview with Colette

Participatory Observation

Co-living with the user, document the behavior

Literature Review

I first started the research by conducting the literature review, in order to understand what the disease is, and what aftermath the patients might have if they ate the wrong food with a high GI index. From online crowdsourcing data, we realized the importance of informing patients the low GI product, summarized the findings and shared the knowledge with the team.

The effects of high GI foods mentioned by an online forum - Insidetracker

On the other hand, we also looked into the existing GI database for technical support. We found that the most complete database is the one provided by the University of Sydney. However, we also noticed that the GI for the same product also can be different in different country's database due to the sugar used and other factors. Therefore, we decided to use the US-based database at this moment.

The GI of whole fat milk can vary according to country

Interview

We then summarized the findings and verified with Colette. Meanwhile, we also did several rounds of a detailed interview with Colette, to verify if we understood the problem that we were tacking and to learn her story with the disease and the medical history. During the interview, she mentioned that:

“I learned a lot of information about low GI food from my mother. But many other people don't know much about it (the disease). I do want to spread what I know to influence the patients like me.”

From this conversation, we realized that it is essential to provide a public channel for people to share their personal experience with specific food with each other, thus creating a crow-sourcing database.

Participatory observation

Align with the literature review and the interview, I organized a participatory observation with Colette from two aspects:

  • 1. Two of our team members went shopping together with Colette, observed and documented her shopping behavior in order to understand what she was mostly concerned about when deciding to buy a product or not.
  • 2. I asked Colette to keep tracking and recording her daily food purchase, plus the corresponding GI number and her recommendation regarding the menu.

Participatory observation = co-shopping + co-documenting

During the shopping, we found that even as a physics Ph.D. student, it was hard for Colette to get the right average GI in her cart instantly. In this way, providing a convenient way for GI look-up and calculation was the core task.

After a month's data collection, I made the persona for Colette and mapped her daily journey related to GI activities. The persona included the basic information of Colette, as well as the average information of her daily meal. The climax appeared around the meal, which was the time that she needed the knowledge and the calculation of GI. It also had a peak at night, during which she answered questions in the GI community.

Created the persona for Colette

Colette's daily activities related to GI

When presented to her, Colette liked the format of the data visualization regarding the GI-a pie chart not only tells the general performance of the GI but also each segment. We considered it a good format guiding our further design.

The visualization of Colette's diary

To sum up, through user research, we understand that:

  • 1. When looking up the food GI, provide as detailed and accurate GI information as possible;
  • 2. When shopping, it is usually inconvenient for the users to type; therefore, we need other interactive methods for GI searching;
  • 3. It is very necessary and valuable to provide an online forum to share GI knowledge.

Co-design

Once we understood the potential of the design, we began to co-design with Colette in terms of the flow and the potential UI. Based on the previous analysis, we decided on four fundamental tabs, namely the dashboard, the dictionary, the calculator, and the forum. We also marked out the key questions that need to be solved further. For example, at which step(s) should we use voice recognition?

The solution we would ultimately choose for looking up also depended on the accuracy of the voice recognition model by the developing team - they were training the model of the GI dictionary parallel to the design exercise. At this stage, we provided both the solution of type-in and voice recognition.

Evaluation

We received some feedback from the juries and other general audience and synthesized them as the following:

  • 1. The sequence of the 4 tabs can be adjusted more logically, e.g. starting with the dashboard;
  • 2. We previously did a lot of data analysis work in terms of the GI numbers, is there a way to represent this nuance on the application? Or do all the users need this information?
  • 3. What is the method of adding the items into the cart for calculation, is it text type-in based, or voice recognition based, or both?

Second Round Design

User Research

Once we collected the feedback of the demo, we then turned into deeper user research with the target questions. In this round, we contacted with the diabetes association and get 4 participants' permission to do the online user research.

Participants

Cover anonymous 4 patients with the same disease

Online

Use the online google meet/doc to introduce the task and document the results

Tasks

Ask the participants to finish three tasks

Refinement

The first task we asked the participants is to do a ranking. In the previous proposal, the tabs are decided to prioritize the functions of dictionary and calculation, since we mainly prototyped these two. In this task, we asked the users to consider the frequency, importance, duration, and other matrices to perform the ranking of these tabs. This task helps us to identify the sequence of the bottom tabs. We then applied weights to the three main matrices and synthesized the findings and adjusted the sequence of the bottom navigation tabs.

Taking the results of the user research into design

The second task we did was a clicking test asking them to find a specific product based on the original design scheme. Through this task, we found that due to the small pool of the domestic database, the GI number between different sub-categories of a product doesn't change drastically.

In this way, instead of letting people search the specific product, which is not accessible under most cases and prolongs the clicking duration, we provide two steps of the look-up. First, we provide the average number of all the products under one category at a high level. For the ones who have available sub-categories, I allow the users to search further. The refined look-up process with different information hierarchy

Finally, we refined the calculation experience. Originally, the experience went from look-up to add and calculation, which means the users would first look up the GI of specific products, and then add them into the cart. In this way, we assume that the items that would be entered into the calculator were already stored in the system through previous steps. In this way, the users only need to pick the item tags stored in the cache in the adding box.

However, when we use the mock-up asking the users to pick the method of adding an item, all of the users pick up voice recognition and typing instead of picking the tag. They mentioned that it was because they already had some knowledge about specific items, thus didn't need to look up them every time. To avoid these edge cases, the adding needs to be independent of previous experience.

Lessons Learned

Through the design process of Colette, I realized that it is very helpful to specify the user instead of designing for someone virtually. Starting from Colette with participatory observation contributes to identifying the key design issues at the very beginning, which paves the way for future design, testing, and implementation.

Meanwhile, keeping the idea of crowd-sourcing data platform is a practical idea which can be applied for many projects, especially under the era of big data. For this project, we are not able to find a perfect U.S.-based dataset with high resolution. In this way, to establish a forum do help the users communicate with each other and build up the GI wiki.
